COVID-19 Data Explorer:
Policies, practices and technology

About

The COVID-19 Data Explorer: Policies, practices and technology supports the discovery and exploration of policies and practices relating to digital vaccine passports and contact tracing apps across the world. The data on timelines, technologies, and public response can be used to explore the legacy and implications of their rapid deployment.

The tool has been developed in partnership with a research design company, Hetco Design, to visualise the data and is accompanied by a synthesis report.

This project builds on work undertaken by the Ada Lovelace Institute in 2020 and 2021 to rapidly track, monitor and understand novel COVID-19 technologies as governments around the world developed and deployed them to manage and respond to the public health crisis.

Our research during the pandemic included workshops and webinars, public attitudes research, evidence reviews and desk research, compiling comprehensive evidence and engaging with experts and the public.

This work resulted in the development of the International monitor: vaccine passports and COVID-19 status apps and the COVID-19 digital contact tracing tracker. Both of these outputs track policies and practices related to these technologies up to October 2021, and have become prominent sources of information for researchers and journalists.

In 2022, as the pandemic eased in many parts of the world, the Ada Lovelace Institute continued to monitor emerging developments. With many countries actively using digital vaccine passports and contact tracing apps, our research shifted focus to investigate whether the risks identified in our previous research had materialised. The data collected and presented in this Explorer demonstrates how countries’ approaches to technology, policy and practice differed and evolved over time.

Methodolodgy

To produce the monitor we collected country-specific quantitative and qualitative data on the following:

  1. Introduction date of digital vaccine passports

  2. Cumulative number of cases

  3. Cumulative number of deaths

  4. Share of vaccinated people

  5. Implementation of digital vaccine passports, e.g., mandatory in workplaces, international travel, etc.

  6. Government-launched contact tracing apps

  7. Features of government-launched contact tracing apps, e.g., the inclusion of vaccine information

  8. Technical infrastructure of contact tracing apps

  9. Evidence of governance

  10. Evidence of technical problems

  11. Evidence of repurposing the technology, e.g., using the data collected through a contact tracing app for the surveillance of citizens

  12. Evidence of long-term implications, e.g., whether a digital vaccine passport has been transformed into a digital identity system

  13. Evidence of protests concerning vaccine passports

Sampling

The COVID-19 Data Explorer focuses on policies and practices in 34 countries in total. Our sampling aims to be regionally representative and is based on the geographical regions of North Africa, Central Africa, South Africa, South East Asia, Central Asia, East Asia, North America, South America, Eastern Europe, European Union, Middle East and Oceania.

Our World in Data datasets on the total deaths, total cases, and the share of fully vaccinated people have been used to create a pandemic impact score for each country, giving equal weight to the three variables. In each geographical region, two countries with a high impact score, two countries with a medium impact score, and two countries with a low impact score were selected for detailed review.

Methods and data

Ada Lovelace Institute datasets

Literature review: Academic and grey literature was reviewed, with findings presented in the country pages.

Desk research: Desk research was conducted using a set of keywords for each country in our sample. These keywords include “vaccine certificate”, “vaccine passport”, “immunity certificate”, “digital contact tracing”, “contact tracing app”, “COVID-19 technologies” and “the name of the country”.

Call for evidence: In July 2022, the Ada Lovelace Institute put out an international call for evidence on the effectiveness and social impact of digital vaccine passports and contact tracing. Relevant evidence is used as case studies, and coded under the following data categories:

  1. Introduction date of digital vaccine passports

  2. Implementation of digital vaccine passports, e.g., mandatory in workplaces, international travel, etc

  3. Government-launched contact tracing apps

  4. Features of government-launched contact tracing apps, e.g., the inclusion of vaccine information

  5. Technical infrastructure of contact tracing apps

  6. Evidence of governance

  7. Evidence of technical problems

  8. Evidence of repurposing the technology, e.g., using the data collected through a contact tracing app for the surveillance of citizens

  9. Evidence of long-term implications, e.g., whether a digital vaccine passport has been transformed into a digital identity system

  10. Evidence of protests concerning vaccine passportss

Limitations and strengths

There are currently 34 countries in this sample. The data aims to be representative, but it was not possible to build comprehensive evidence for every continent, country, or region.

The evidence on COVID-19 technologies is sparse. In order to address these limitations a variety of sources were used, including media outlets, research papers, government websites, and the evidence submitted to Ada. Nevertheless, the limited evidence base indicates the lack of in-depth evaluation and research on these novel technologies at an international level.

As a UK-based organisation, there may be evidence from parts of the world which we have been unable to access.

A language barrier also emerged in policy analysis. Our methodology was to conduct research in the official languages of the countries in our sample, collecting data on policies and regulations from government websites and policy papers and using Google Translate. However, this was challenging, and it was often difficult to source official policy papers. Our country-specific data on governance, legacy and public attitudes draws on media reports and research papers in the English language.

Despite these limitations, the COVID-19 Data Explorer offers a valuable synopsis of when these technologies were rolled out and how they have been governed across the world. The Ada Lovelace Institute will continue to monitor the uses and developments of these technologies and update our data periodically. This tool is a valuable source of information for civil society, researchers and policymakers as they continue to research pandemic technologies.

We welcome the contributions of researchers, journalists, experts, and the public to help us update policies and improve our research. Please contact us.
